How it works

Understanding standard deviation

s

Sample vs population

Use sample (n−1) when your data is a sample drawn from a larger process — which is almost always the case in quality and manufacturing. Use population (n) only when you have every single data point from a closed, finite group. The n−1 denominator corrects for bias in estimating the true population variance.

CV

Coefficient of variation

The coefficient of variation (CV = σ/μ × 100%) expresses standard deviation as a percentage of the mean. This lets you compare variability across processes with different scales. A CV below 10% is generally low variation; above 30% signals high relative variability.

68-95

The empirical rule

For data that is approximately normally distributed: 68% of values fall within ±1σ of the mean, 95% within ±2σ, and 99.7% within ±3σ. If your data deviates significantly from these expectations, it may be non-normal — consider a Box Plot or normality test before applying Cp/Cpk analysis.

About this calculator

What this standard deviation calculator does

Standard deviation is the most-used measure of spread in statistics. It expresses the typical distance between a data point and the mean in the same units as the original data. Larger SD = more variability = wider distribution. This calculator takes a paste-in list of numbers and returns the count, mean, range, variance, sample standard deviation (dividing by n-1), population standard deviation (dividing by n), and the coefficient of variation (SD ÷ mean × 100%) — a unit-free measure useful for comparing variability across processes that operate at different scales.

When to use it

Use this calculator any time you have raw measurements and need a single number for spread: capability studies, control limit calculations, sample-size planning, hypothesis testing, A/B comparisons or supplier benchmarking. It is also the right tool when somebody quotes a mean without an SD — variability is usually the more important half of the story.

Worked example

A sample of 15 weights gives a mean of 250 g with sample SD = 12 g and range = 48 g. The coefficient of variation is 12 ÷ 250 × 100 = 4.8% — a tight process. By comparison, a competing process with the same mean but SD = 30 g has CV = 12% — three times more variable, even though both produce the same average weight. Cpk for the same spec window will be roughly three times higher on the first process; the customer will see far fewer out-of-spec parts.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between sample and population SD?

Sample SD divides by n-1 (Bessel correction) and is the right choice whenever your data is a sample drawn from a larger population — which is almost every real-world dataset. Population SD divides by n and is only used when you have measured every member of the population.

How does standard deviation differ from variance?

Variance is the average squared deviation from the mean; standard deviation is its square root. SD is in the same units as the original data (grams, seconds, pounds), which makes it easier to interpret. Variance is more useful in formulae because variances of independent variables add.

What is the coefficient of variation used for?

CV (SD ÷ mean × 100%) is a unit-free measure of relative variability, useful for comparing the spread of two processes that operate at different scales. CV < 10% is tight; 10-30% is moderate; > 30% indicates a process where variability dominates the mean.

Is SD affected by outliers?

Yes — strongly. A single extreme value can inflate the SD by 50% or more on a small sample, because deviations are squared before averaging. For data with known outliers, use the median absolute deviation (MAD) or the IQR as a robust alternative measure of spread.

How do I interpret SD on a normal distribution?

For normal data, about 68% of values fall within ±1 SD of the mean, 95% within ±2 SD and 99.7% within ±3 SD. This empirical rule is the basis of control limits, capability indices and Six Sigma defect-rate calculations — and only holds when the data is approximately normal.
