How it works

Understanding Correlation

1

Reading r

Pearson's r ranges from -1 to +1. Values near 0 mean little or no linear relationship; near +1 means a strong positive linear relationship; near -1 means a strong negative relationship. R² (r squared) tells you what fraction of the variance in Y is explained by X.

2

Correlation ≠ causation

A high correlation never proves cause and effect. The two variables may both be driven by a third unmeasured factor, or the relationship may be coincidental. Always pair correlation analysis with process knowledge before acting.

3

Watch for non-linearity

Pearson r only measures linear relationships. Two variables with a strong curved relationship can have r near zero. Always look at the scatter plot — if the cloud of points isn't roughly straight, consider transformation or a non-linear method.

About this calculator

What this correlation calculator does

The Pearson correlation coefficient r measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables. It runs from -1 (perfect negative linear relationship) through 0 (no linear relationship) to +1 (perfect positive linear relationship). This calculator takes paired x/y values, returns Pearson's r, the coefficient of determination R² (the share of variance in y explained by x), the sample size, and a scatter plot so you can see the relationship rather than just read the number. It also classifies the strength using the standard thresholds (|r| < 0.3 weak, 0.3-0.7 moderate, > 0.7 strong) and warns when sample size is too small for the correlation to be reliable.

When to use it

Use this calculator during the Analyse phase of DMAIC when investigating possible cause-and-effect relationships, during root-cause analysis, when sizing the prize from controlling a process variable, or before fitting a regression model. It is also the right tool when somebody claims two metrics "go together" and you want to test the claim with actual numbers.

Worked example

A study of 30 bakeries pairs daily oven temperature (°C) with daily defect rate (%). Pearson r = -0.78 — a strong negative correlation: hotter ovens, fewer defects. R² = 0.61, meaning oven temperature explains 61% of the variation in defects. The scatter plot confirms a clean, near-linear relationship with no obvious outliers. This justifies a confirmation trial and, if confirmed, a control plan that holds oven temperature within a tighter range.

Frequently asked questions

What does the correlation coefficient r mean?

Pearson's r quantifies the linear relationship between two variables. It is dimensionless and bounded between -1 and +1. The sign shows direction; the magnitude shows strength. r = 0 means no linear relationship — but a strong non-linear relationship can still be present.

What is the difference between r and R-squared?

R² is simply r squared. While r can be negative (showing direction), R² is always between 0 and 1 and represents the proportion of variance in y that is linearly explained by x. R² = 0.49 means 49% of the variance in y is associated with x.

Does correlation imply causation?

No. A strong correlation tells you the two variables move together but says nothing about which (if either) causes the other. There may be a third lurking variable, the relationship may be coincidental, or the direction may be reversed. Causation requires a designed experiment.

How big a sample do I need to trust a correlation?

A bare minimum of 30 paired observations gives a usable estimate; 100+ is much more reliable. With small samples, even strong-looking correlations can be statistical noise — always look at the scatter plot and the statistical significance, not just the r value.

What should I do when correlation is weak but I expected it to be strong?

Plot the data. The relationship may be non-linear (try Spearman rank correlation or a transformation), the data may contain outliers distorting r, or the variables may simply not be related. Splitting by subgroup sometimes reveals strong correlations within groups that wash out in aggregate.
