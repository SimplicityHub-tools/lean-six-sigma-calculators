How it works

Understanding Takt Time

1

The heartbeat of production

Takt time sets the pace at which one unit must be completed to satisfy demand. If takt time is 2 minutes, the process must produce one unit every 2 minutes to avoid building a backlog.

2

Balancing to takt

Cycle time should be at or just below takt time at each step. Steps running faster than takt create inventory. Steps running slower become bottlenecks and will fail to meet demand.

3

Takt time changes

Takt time is not fixed — it shifts as demand changes or as available time changes due to overtime, extra shifts or reduced production days. Recalculate whenever either input changes.

About this calculator

What this takt time calculator does

Takt time is the heartbeat of a Lean process — the rate at which one unit must be completed to keep up with customer demand, no faster and no slower. The word comes from the German "Takt", meaning beat or rhythm. The formula is simple: net available production time divided by customer demand in the same window. This calculator takes your shift length, planned breaks, planned downtime and the number of units the customer wants in that period, and returns takt time in seconds per unit, units per hour and units per shift. Compare it against your measured cycle time and you instantly know whether the process is balanced, behind demand, or quietly overproducing.

When to use it

Use this calculator at the start of any line-balancing exercise, value-stream map, capacity quote or new-product introduction. It is also the right tool when sales takes on a bigger contract and you need to decide whether the existing line can absorb the extra demand or whether a second shift, additional operator or kaizen event is required.

Worked example

A line runs 8 hours per shift with 30 minutes of planned breaks and 15 minutes of planned changeover. Net available time = 480 - 30 - 15 = 435 minutes = 26,100 seconds. Customer demand = 290 units per shift. Takt time = 26,100 ÷ 290 = 90 seconds per unit. If your measured cycle time is 110 seconds, the line is 22% behind demand and you have a balancing problem. If cycle time is 70 seconds, you are overproducing by 22% and risk building inventory.

Frequently asked questions

What is takt time in Lean?

Takt time is the rate at which units must be produced to exactly meet customer demand. It is calculated as net available production time divided by customer demand for the same period. It sets the pace the process should run at — no faster, no slower.

How is takt time different from cycle time?

Takt time is set by the customer (the demand rate). Cycle time is set by the process (how fast it actually produces). Takt time tells you how fast you should be running; cycle time tells you how fast you are running. The gap between them is the improvement opportunity.

What happens if cycle time exceeds takt time?

You cannot meet demand on a single shift. Options are line balancing, removing waste from the longest step (the bottleneck), running overtime, adding a shift, or reducing the customer commitment. Doing nothing leads to late deliveries, expedited freight and customer escalations.

Should takt time include breaks and downtime?

Use net available time — gross shift time minus planned breaks, scheduled meetings and planned changeovers. Do not subtract unplanned downtime; that is an OEE issue, not a takt-time issue, and excluding it would hide the real performance gap.

How often should takt time be recalculated?

Recalculate any time customer demand changes by more than ~10%, or when shift patterns change. Many high-mix operations update takt time monthly or even weekly. A stale takt time is the most common reason a once-balanced line drifts out of balance.
