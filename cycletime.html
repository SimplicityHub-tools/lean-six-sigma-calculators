Understanding Cycle Time

1

Actual vs ideal

Cycle time is the real, measured time to make one unit — including any small variations or stoppages. Compared to takt time, it tells you whether your process is keeping up with demand or overproducing.

2

Cycle < Takt

If cycle time is shorter than takt, you are producing faster than demand requires — which builds inventory and ties up capital. Slow the process or reallocate operators to other value streams.

3

Cycle > Takt

If cycle time is longer than takt, this step is the bottleneck — it can't keep up with demand. Reduce the cycle time (kaizen, SMED, automation) or add capacity at this station.

About this calculator

What this cycle time calculator does

Cycle time is the actual, measured time it takes a process to produce one unit, end-to-end. It is one of the four foundational Lean metrics — alongside takt time, lead time and throughput — and the single most useful number for spotting bottlenecks on a production line, in an office workflow or in a service operation. This calculator takes the net production time available (after planned breaks, changeovers and downtime) and the number of good units actually completed in that window, and returns cycle time in seconds, minutes or hours per unit. If you also enter your takt time, the tool tells you whether the process is overproducing, balanced, or behind demand.

When to use it

Use this calculator whenever you need a clean, defensible cycle-time figure for a Lean assessment, an A3, a Value Stream Map, an OEE study or a customer-facing capacity quotation. It is also the right tool when you suspect a step is the bottleneck and want a number to back up the hypothesis before launching a kaizen event.

Worked example

A packing line runs for 480 minutes of net time per shift and produces 240 good cases. Cycle time = 480 × 60 ÷ 240 = 120 seconds per unit, or 30 cases per hour. If takt time is 100 seconds per unit, this step is the bottleneck (cycle > takt by 20%) and is the right place to spend SMED, 5S or kaizen effort.

Frequently asked questions

What is cycle time in Lean manufacturing?

Cycle time is the actual, measured time taken to complete one unit of work, including any small variations or stoppages within the process. It is different from theoretical or ideal cycle time, which assumes the process never slows or stops.

How is cycle time different from takt time?

Takt time is the rate at which units must be produced to meet customer demand (available time ÷ demand). Cycle time is how fast the process actually produces them. If cycle time is shorter than takt time you are overproducing; if cycle time exceeds takt time the step is a bottleneck.

How do you reduce cycle time?

The four most effective levers are: SMED to cut changeover time, 5S to remove walking and searching waste, line balancing to redistribute work between operators, and small batch sizes to reduce queue time. Automation is usually the last lever, not the first.

What does cycle time tell you about capacity?

Capacity per shift = available time ÷ cycle time, multiplied by OEE. A cycle time of 60 seconds with 8 hours of available time and 75% OEE gives a realistic capacity of 360 units per shift.

Is cycle time the same as lead time?

No. Cycle time measures one unit through one process step. Lead time measures one unit through the entire value stream, including all queues and waits between steps. Lead time is almost always many times longer than cycle time.
