How it works

Understanding Cost of Poor Quality

1

Four cost categories

Quality costs split into four buckets. Internal and external failure are pure waste. Appraisal is checking — necessary but not value-added. Prevention is the only category that actually reduces total COPQ when you spend more on it.

2

Typical benchmarks

Untouched processes often run at 15-25% of revenue in COPQ — most of it hidden in scrap, rework and inspection. World-class operations hold COPQ below 2% of revenue. The gap between those two figures is usually pure profit waiting to be unlocked.

3

Shift the spend

The fastest way to cut COPQ is to invest more in prevention and less in failure response. Every £1 spent on prevention typically saves £4-£10 in failure costs — but only if the prevention spend is targeted at the actual root causes of defects.

About this calculator

What this cost of poor quality calculator does

Cost of Poor Quality (COPQ) is every pound a business spends because work is not done right first time. It splits into four categories: internal failure (scrap, rework, downtime caught before despatch), external failure (warranty, returns, complaints, lost customers), appraisal (inspection, testing, audits) and prevention (training, process design, supplier development). This calculator sums the four buckets into a single annual figure, expresses it as a percentage of revenue, and benchmarks against the world-class threshold of 2%. Untouched processes typically run at 15-25% of revenue in COPQ — almost all of which is hidden in scrap, rework and inspection budgets that nobody adds up.

When to use it

Use this calculator when scoping a Six Sigma programme, building the business case for a quality management system, justifying investment in prevention, or answering the board question "what is poor quality actually costing us?". It is also the right tool when comparing two sites or two product families to decide where to deploy improvement resource first.

Worked example

A £2m turnover SME has £60k internal failure, £40k external failure, £30k appraisal and £20k prevention spend per year. Total COPQ = £150k, or 7.5% of revenue. The gap to a 2%-of-revenue benchmark (£40k) is £110k of recoverable margin. Failure costs (£100k) are five times prevention spend (£20k), so the highest-payback move is shifting some inspection budget into root-cause prevention work — every £1 spent on targeted prevention typically saves £4-£10 in failure cost.

Frequently asked questions

What is Cost of Poor Quality (COPQ)?

COPQ is the total cost a business carries because products or services are not delivered defect-free first time. It includes both visible costs (scrap, rework, warranty) and hidden costs (extra inspection, lost customers, management time).

What are the four COPQ categories?

Internal failure (defects caught before the customer), external failure (defects that escape to the customer), appraisal (the cost of checking) and prevention (the cost of stopping defects from happening). Only prevention spend reduces total COPQ as it grows.

What is a typical COPQ as a percentage of revenue?

Untouched processes typically run at 15-25% of revenue. Average industry performance is 5-10%. World-class operations hold COPQ below 2% of revenue. Any business above 5% almost certainly has a profitable Six Sigma programme waiting.

What is the prevention-failure ratio?

A useful diagnostic: divide prevention spend by failure spend. Healthy programmes run 1:1 or higher. Most untouched businesses run 1:10 or worse, which is why their COPQ stays high — they spend on inspection rather than on stopping defects at source.

How quickly does prevention spend pay back?

Targeted prevention spend (root-cause analysis, mistake-proofing, supplier development) typically pays back in 6-12 months at a 4-10x ratio. Untargeted prevention spend (generic training with no follow-through) often does not pay back at all — the targeting matters more than the amount.
