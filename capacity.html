How it works

Understanding Production Capacity

1

Theoretical vs realistic

Dividing available time by cycle time gives theoretical capacity. Multiplying by OEE adjusts for real-world losses — downtime, slow running and quality defects — to give a realistic, dependable capacity figure.

2

OEE is the lever

You can usually lift capacity faster by improving OEE than by adding shifts or buying machines. Lift OEE from 60% to 75% and you've added 25% capacity at zero capital cost — and reduced cost-per-unit at the same time.

3

Match capacity to demand

If capacity is well above demand, you have headroom but may be over-resourced. If capacity is below demand, you have a capacity gap that needs improvement, extra shifts, or new equipment. The capacity-vs-demand check tells you which situation you're in.

About this calculator

What this production capacity calculator does

Production capacity is how many good units a process can realistically deliver in a given time window. The naïve formula — available time divided by cycle time — gives the theoretical maximum, but every real process loses output to downtime, micro-stops, slow running and quality defects. This calculator multiplies the theoretical figure by OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness) to give a realistic, dependable capacity in units per shift, per day and per year. If you also enter your daily demand, the tool tells you whether you have headroom, are just covering it, or have a capacity gap.

When to use it

Use this calculator whenever you are quoting customer lead times, deciding whether to add a shift, costing a new product, planning a peak-season ramp, or building the business case for an OEE improvement programme. It is also the right tool when sales asks "can we take on this contract?" and you need a defensible answer in the next ten minutes.

Worked example

A packaging line has 8 hours of available time per shift, a 60-second cycle time and runs at 75% OEE. Theoretical capacity = 8 × 3600 ÷ 60 = 480 units. Realistic capacity = 480 × 0.75 = 360 units per shift. If demand is 400 units per day on a single shift, you have a capacity gap of 40 units (90% coverage) — and lifting OEE from 75% to 85% would close it without adding people or machines.

Frequently asked questions

What is production capacity?

Production capacity is the maximum output a process can sustainably deliver in a given time period. The realistic figure is theoretical capacity multiplied by OEE, expressed in good units per shift, day, week or year.

How does OEE affect capacity?

OEE captures the three real-world losses — availability, performance and quality — that prevent a process from achieving its theoretical maximum. A line with 85% OEE delivers 41% more capacity than one with 60% OEE running on the same equipment.

What is the difference between theoretical and realistic capacity?

Theoretical capacity assumes zero downtime, zero defects and the ideal cycle time. Realistic capacity adjusts that figure by OEE to reflect actual losses. Quoting customers from theoretical capacity is the most common cause of broken delivery promises.

How do you calculate annual capacity?

Multiply realistic per-shift capacity by the number of shifts per day, then by the number of working days per year (typically 250 in the UK after weekends and bank holidays). This calculator does the per-day and per-year maths automatically.

Should I add a shift or improve OEE?

Improving OEE is almost always cheaper. A 10-point OEE lift adds the same capacity as a third of a shift but with no extra labour, energy or supervision cost — and reduces cost-per-unit at the same time.
