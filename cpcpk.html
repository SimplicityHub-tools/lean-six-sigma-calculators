How it works

Understanding Cp and Cpk

Cp

Potential capability

Cp measures how much of the specification range your process variation occupies — assuming the process is perfectly centred. A Cp of 1.0 means variation exactly fills the spec range. Higher is better.

Cpk

Actual capability

Cpk accounts for where the process mean sits relative to the specification limits. It is always equal to or lower than Cp. A large gap between Cp and Cpk means the process is off-centre.

✓

Target values

Most industries require a minimum Cpk of 1.33 for an established process and 1.67 for a new one. A Cpk below 1.0 means the process is producing output outside specification limits.

About this calculator

What this cp / cpk calculator does

Cp and Cpk are the two most-used short-term process capability indices in Six Sigma. Cp measures the potential capability — how the spread of the process compares to the spread of the specification, assuming the process is perfectly centred. Cpk measures the actual capability, accounting for any drift of the process mean away from the centre of the spec. The formulae use the short-term standard deviation (within-subgroup variation) and require the data to be reasonably normal and stable. This calculator takes your upper and lower spec limits, the process mean, and the short-term standard deviation, and returns Cp, Cpu, Cpl, Cpk, the predicted defect rate per million, and a benchmark against the standard thresholds (1.33 capable, 1.67 highly capable, 2.0 six-sigma).

When to use it

Use this calculator after a capability study (typically 100+ measurements collected from a stable process), before signing off a new product, qualifying a supplier, releasing a process for production, or as the C-step deliverable in a Six Sigma project. It is also the right tool when a customer requires a documented Cpk on every shipped batch.

Worked example

A bottle-fill process has spec limits of 495-505 ml, a mean of 500.5 ml and a short-term SD of 1.2 ml. Cp = (505 - 495) / (6 × 1.2) = 1.39 (capable on spread). Cpu = (505 - 500.5) / (3 × 1.2) = 1.25; Cpl = (500.5 - 495) / (3 × 1.2) = 1.53. Cpk = min(Cpu, Cpl) = 1.25 — the upper spec is the limiting side because the mean is slightly high. Centring the process on 500 ml would lift Cpk from 1.25 to the full 1.39 with no reduction in variability.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between Cp and Cpk?

Cp assumes the process is perfectly centred between the spec limits. Cpk takes the smaller of two one-sided capability indices, so it penalises any drift of the mean away from centre. Cp is always greater than or equal to Cpk; they are equal only when the process is perfectly centred.

What Cpk is considered acceptable?

1.33 is the standard "capable" threshold (≈63 defects per million). 1.67 is highly capable (≈0.6 ppm). 2.0 is the classic six-sigma target (3.4 ppm allowing for the 1.5σ shift). Below 1.0 means the process spread exceeds the spec width — defects are inevitable until variability is reduced.

What is the difference between Cpk and Ppk?

Cpk uses the short-term (within-subgroup) standard deviation, capturing only common-cause variation. Ppk uses the overall standard deviation, capturing both within- and between-subgroup variation. Ppk is always less than or equal to Cpk; the gap reveals long-term drift and shift.

How many measurements do I need for capability analysis?

A minimum of 30 measurements gives a usable estimate; 100 or more is recommended for a defensible Cpk. The data should come from a process that is in statistical control — capability calculated on an unstable process is meaningless.

What if my data is not normally distributed?

Standard Cp/Cpk formulae assume normality. For non-normal data, either transform the data (Box-Cox, Johnson) or use a non-normal capability method (percentile-based Cpk using the 0.135% and 99.865% quantiles). Reporting normal-based Cpk on skewed data understates the defect rate.
