How it works

Understanding Pareto Chart

1

The 80/20 rule

The Pareto principle observes that for many phenomena, roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. In quality and process improvement, that means a small number of categories typically drive the majority of defects, downtime or cost.

2

Why ranking matters

Sorting causes from largest to smallest, with a cumulative line drawn over the bars, instantly reveals where to focus. Tackling the first 2-3 bars usually delivers more than working through the rest of the list combined.

3

Quantity vs cost Pareto

A defect category that occurs often may not be the most expensive. It is often worth running two Paretos — one by frequency, one by cost — and combining the insights to pick the highest-value improvement target.

About this calculator

What this pareto chart calculator does

A Pareto chart is the simplest, fastest tool in the Lean Six Sigma kit for spotting where to focus. Built on the Pareto principle — that roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes — it ranks categories from largest to smallest with a cumulative line drawn over the bars, instantly revealing the "vital few" that drive most of the problem. This calculator takes your raw data (one category per line, with its count or cost), sorts and ranks it, and renders a clean Pareto chart with the 80% threshold marked. It also returns the count of vital-few categories, the total observation count, and the share of the largest contributor.

When to use it

Use this calculator at the start of any improvement project: defect analysis, downtime analysis, complaint analysis, cost analysis, scrap analysis, late-delivery analysis. It is also the right tool when a meeting is heading toward solving every category at once — a Pareto refocuses the conversation onto the 20% that matters.

Worked example

A workshop logs scratches (45), misalignment (28), loose fasteners (22), wrong colour (14), missing label (9) and other (7). Total = 125 defects. Ranked, scratches alone are 36%, misalignment lifts cumulative to 58%, loose fasteners to 76%, wrong colour to 87%. Just three categories cause over 80% — a classic 80/20 distribution. The improvement plan should target scratches first, misalignment second, loose fasteners third, and ignore the long tail until those three are solved.

Frequently asked questions

What is a Pareto chart used for?

A Pareto chart ranks categories from largest to smallest with a cumulative line, making it instantly clear which few categories drive the majority of effects. It is used for defect, cost, complaint, downtime and root-cause analysis.

What is the Pareto principle?

The Pareto principle, sometimes called the 80/20 rule, observes that roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. It is empirical, not exact — but holds remarkably well across quality, sales, defects, complaints and time use.

How do you build a Pareto chart by hand?

Sort categories from largest to smallest. Draw a bar for each. Calculate the cumulative percentage as you move left to right and plot a line over the bars. Mark a dashed horizontal line at 80%. The categories below that line are the vital few.

Should I Pareto by frequency or by cost?

Run both. A defect that occurs often may not be the most expensive, and a rare defect can be ruinous. Combining a frequency Pareto with a cost-weighted Pareto usually picks a different — and higher-value — improvement target than either alone.

What if my data does not show 80/20?

Some processes are genuinely flat: every category contributes roughly equally. That is itself a useful finding — it tells you a single focus area will not work, and you need parallel, smaller actions across many categories instead.
