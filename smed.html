How it works

Understanding SMED Changeover ROI

1

SMED in one line

Single-Minute Exchange of Die (SMED) is a structured method to cut changeover time, ideally below 10 minutes. The technique separates internal steps (machine stopped) from external steps (machine running), then converts as many internal steps as possible to external.

2

Why changeovers matter

Long changeovers force big batches, which mean more inventory, longer lead times, and slower customer response. Cutting changeovers shrinks economic batch size — letting you run smaller, faster batches with the same machine and people.

3

The hidden gain

The headline gain is recovered minutes, but the bigger win is flexibility — being able to run any product on demand, with negligible setup cost. That collapses lead times, reduces forecast errors and frees up cash tied in finished-goods inventory.

About this calculator

What this smed roi calculator does

SMED — Single-Minute Exchange of Die — is the structured Lean method for cutting changeover time, ideally to under ten minutes. Pioneered by Shigeo Shingo at Toyota, the technique separates internal setup steps (which can only happen with the machine stopped) from external setup steps (which can be done while the machine is still running), then converts as many internal steps as possible into external ones. This calculator turns a SMED proposal into pounds. Enter your current and target changeover times, how often you change over, your production rate and your contribution per unit, and the tool returns minutes recovered per day, extra units per day, and the annual revenue gain from running the same equipment with the same people.

When to use it

Use this calculator when building the business case for a SMED workshop, pitching a kaizen event to a finance director, sizing the prize before committing engineering time, or comparing two competing improvement projects. It is also the right tool when a board member asks "what would faster changeovers actually be worth?".

Worked example

A bottling line currently changes over in 60 minutes, four times per day. After SMED, target is 15 minutes. Time saved = (60-15) × 4 = 180 minutes per day. At 2 units per minute, that is 360 extra units per day or roughly 90,000 per year. At £5 contribution per unit, that is £450,000 in additional annual revenue from the same machine, same labour, and the same shift pattern — a 75% changeover reduction.

Frequently asked questions

What does SMED stand for?

SMED stands for Single-Minute Exchange of Die. The "single minute" refers to the single-digit target — under ten minutes — for any changeover, regardless of how complex it appears today.

How long does a SMED workshop take?

A typical SMED kaizen event runs for three to five days: one day to film and analyse the current changeover, two days to redesign and trial the improved method, and one to two days to standardise, train and document the new procedure.

What is the difference between internal and external setup?

Internal setup steps can only be done while the machine is stopped (e.g. removing a tool). External steps can be done while the machine is still running the previous job (e.g. fetching the next tool, pre-heating, pre-staging materials). Converting internal to external is the heart of SMED.

What is a realistic SMED reduction?

Most first-pass SMED workshops achieve a 50-75% reduction in changeover time. World-class operations sustain reductions of 90%+ over multiple iterations, often hitting single-minute changeover on processes that originally took hours.

What is the hidden benefit of SMED beyond time?

The bigger prize is flexibility. Faster changeovers shrink economic batch size, which collapses lead times, reduces forecast error, frees up cash tied in finished-goods inventory, and lets you run smaller, more frequent batches in response to real demand.
