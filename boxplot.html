How it works

Reading a box plot

IQR

The box — middle 50%

The box spans from Q1 (25th percentile) to Q3 (75th percentile). This is the Interquartile Range (IQR) — the spread of the middle 50% of your data. A narrow box means data is concentrated. A wide box means high variability in the core of the dataset.

1.5×

Whiskers — the expected range

The whiskers extend to the most extreme values still within 1.5 × IQR of Q1 and Q3. Any data point beyond the whiskers is flagged as an outlier. Outliers are not errors by definition — they may represent real events worth investigating.

⟂

Skewness — distribution shape

If the median is near the centre of the box, the data is roughly symmetric. If it is closer to Q1, the distribution is right-skewed (long upper tail). Closer to Q3 means left-skewed. Comparing the mean and median also reveals skewness direction and magnitude.

About this calculator

What this box plot calculator does

The box plot — also called the box-and-whisker plot — is the fastest way to see the shape, spread and outliers of a sample at a glance. It encodes the 5-number summary (minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum) into a compact visual: a box from Q1 to Q3, a line at the median, whiskers extending to the most extreme values within 1.5×IQR of the quartiles, and individual points for any values outside that range (potential outliers). This calculator takes your raw data, returns the 5-number summary plus the IQR and a list of flagged outliers, and renders a clean box plot. Compare two or more groups side-by-side to see differences in centre, spread and tail behaviour without running a formal test.

When to use it

Use this calculator early in any data analysis: before running a t-test or ANOVA (to check the assumption of similar spreads), at the Measure stage of DMAIC, in QA acceptance reviews, or any time you need to communicate the shape of a sample to a non-statistical audience. It is also the right tool when comparing performance across operators, shifts, suppliers or product variants.

Worked example

A sample of 25 cycle times yields min = 38, Q1 = 44, median = 47, Q3 = 51, max = 68. IQR = 51 - 44 = 7. Upper whisker limit = 51 + 1.5 × 7 = 61.5; lower whisker limit = 44 - 1.5 × 7 = 33.5. Two values (65 and 68) sit above the upper whisker and are flagged as outliers — both correlate with a known fixture issue at the start of shift 3, confirming a special cause rather than natural variation. Without the outliers the maximum drops to 56 and the data looks much tighter.

Frequently asked questions

What is the 1.5×IQR outlier rule?

Any value below Q1 - 1.5×IQR or above Q3 + 1.5×IQR is flagged as a potential outlier. The factor 1.5 was chosen by Tukey to flag roughly 0.7% of values from a normal distribution — a useful first filter, not a hard rule. Some applications use 3×IQR for "extreme" outliers.

What does a box plot show that summary statistics cannot?

Skew (asymmetry of the box around the median, or asymmetric whisker length), tail behaviour (whisker length and outliers), and direct visual comparison between groups. A mean and SD can be identical for very different distributions — the box plot exposes the difference instantly.

How is a box plot different from a histogram?

A histogram shows the full shape of the distribution but needs more data to be meaningful (50+ values). A box plot summarises the shape into five numbers and works well even on small samples (10-30 values). Use box plots to compare groups; use histograms to inspect a single distribution in detail.

Should I always remove flagged outliers?

No. Investigate them first. If the outlier is a measurement error, transcription error or known special cause, remove it. If it is a real, valid value (rare event, edge case, important customer), keep it — those values often contain the most useful information about process limits.

Can box plots be used to compare more than two groups?

Yes — and this is where they shine. Stacking 4-8 box plots side by side reveals at a glance which group has the highest median, the widest spread, the most outliers, or systematic skew. It often makes a formal ANOVA result intuitively obvious before the test is run.
