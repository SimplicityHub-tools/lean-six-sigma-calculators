How it works

Understanding p-values

p

What a p-value is

The p-value is the probability of observing results at least as extreme as yours, if the null hypothesis were true. It is not the probability that H₀ is true, nor the probability that the result is due to chance. A small p-value (typically <0.05) means the data would be unlikely under H₀, giving evidence to reject it.

α

Significance level vs p-value

The significance level α (commonly 0.05 or 0.01) is set before collecting data. You reject H₀ when p < α. Choosing α=0.05 means you accept a 5% chance of a false positive (Type I error). A smaller α reduces false positives but increases the risk of missing a real effect (Type II error, power).

6σ

Hypothesis testing in Six Sigma

In DMAIC Analyse and Improve phases, hypothesis tests determine whether a factor has a statistically significant effect on the output. Common tests: one-sample t (is the mean at target?), two-sample t (do two groups differ?), chi-square (are defect counts independent of category?). P < 0.05 is standard; for critical decisions use α = 0.01.

About this calculator

What this p-value calculator does

The p-value is the workhorse of hypothesis testing. It is the probability, assuming the null hypothesis is true, of seeing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed. A small p-value (typically below 0.05) is taken as evidence to reject the null. This calculator handles the three most common test statistics — Student t, standard normal Z and chi-square — and returns one-tailed and two-tailed p-values along with a clear significance verdict at the 0.10, 0.05 and 0.01 levels. It also reminds you that statistical significance is not the same as practical significance, and shows the test statistic on the relevant distribution curve.

When to use it

Use this calculator after running any hypothesis test (means, proportions, variances, goodness-of-fit, independence) where you need to convert a test statistic into a probability. It is also the right tool when a colleague reports "p = 0.051 — not significant" and you want to think about whether a slightly different sample size or one-tailed framing changes the conclusion.

Worked example

A two-sample t-test on cycle times produces t = 2.34 with df = 38. The two-tailed p-value is 0.0246, which is below 0.05 → reject the null at the 5% level. The same t and df give a one-tailed p of 0.0123. If the original hypothesis was directional ("the new method is faster"), the one-tailed test is the right read and the result is significant at the 1% level too. With df = 200 the p-value drops further; with df = 8 the p-value rises above 0.05 — sample size always matters.

Frequently asked questions

What does a p-value actually represent?

The p-value is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one in your sample, assuming the null hypothesis is true. A small p-value means the observed data is unlikely under the null and provides evidence to reject it.

What is a significant p-value?

By convention, p < 0.05 is "significant", p < 0.01 is "highly significant", and p < 0.001 is "very highly significant". These thresholds are convention, not law — choose them in advance based on the cost of a false positive in your specific decision.

What is the difference between one- and two-tailed tests?

A two-tailed test asks "is the parameter different from the null value, in either direction?". A one-tailed test asks "is it greater than" or "is it less than". The one-tailed p-value is half the two-tailed p-value. Choose one- vs two-tailed before looking at the data.

Does a non-significant p-value mean there is no effect?

No. It only means there is not enough evidence in your sample to reject the null. The effect may still exist but be too small for your sample size to detect. Always pair a non-significant result with a power analysis or confidence interval before concluding "no difference".

Is a p-value the same as the probability the null is true?

No — this is the most common misinterpretation. The p-value is conditional on the null being true. The probability the null is true given the data requires Bayesian methods and a prior probability. Stating "there is a 5% chance the null is true" from p = 0.05 is incorrect.
