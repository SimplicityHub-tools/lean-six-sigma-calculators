Understanding Rolled Throughput Yield

1

Why steps multiply

The probability of a unit passing through every step defect-free is the product of each step's yield. Five steps at 95% each look fine individually but only deliver 77% RTY — a quarter of units have a defect somewhere.

2

Hidden factory

RTY exposes the "hidden factory" of rework, scrap and inspection that plant-level yield numbers usually mask. Final yield can be 99% only because rework loops are quietly catching upstream defects.

3

Where to focus

The weakest step has the biggest leverage on RTY. Lifting the worst step from 90% to 98% will improve RTY more than any other change — even tiny improvements at the worst step beat moderate ones at already-strong steps.

About this calculator

What this rolled throughput yield calculator does

Rolled Throughput Yield (RTY) is the probability that a single unit passes through every step of a process without defect or rework. Unlike a final-inspection yield, RTY multiplies the first-pass yield of every step in series, exposing the "hidden factory" of rework, retest and selective scrap that final yield masks. This calculator takes the first-pass yield of each step, multiplies them, and returns the cumulative RTY along with the total defect probability and the equivalent Sigma Level. A clean 95% looks good — until you discover that ten 95% steps in series produce a real first-pass yield of just 60%.

When to use it

Use this calculator at the Define or Measure stage of any multi-step process improvement project, before signing off a new product introduction, or any time final yield looks healthy but operators are reporting heavy rework. It is also the right tool when arguing for end-to-end DMAIC scope rather than tackling one step at a time.

Worked example

An assembly process has six steps with first-pass yields of 99%, 98%, 97%, 96%, 95% and 94%. RTY = 0.99 × 0.98 × 0.97 × 0.96 × 0.95 × 0.94 = 0.807, or 80.7%. Roughly 1 in 5 units needs rework somewhere in the line — even though every step in isolation looks "fine". Lifting just the worst step (94%) to 99% would push RTY from 80.7% to 85.0%, recovering 4.3 percentage points of margin without touching the other five steps.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between RTY and final yield?

Final yield only counts what passes the final inspection, regardless of how much rework happened along the way. RTY is the probability of a unit passing every step the first time, with no rework. RTY is always lower than final yield — and the gap is the size of your hidden factory.

What is the hidden factory?

The hidden factory is the unmeasured cost of rework, retest, sort, scrap and re-handling that lives between formal process steps. It consumes 15-30% of capacity in many manufacturing operations and almost never appears on any KPI dashboard.

How do I measure first-pass yield for each step?

Count units entering the step and units leaving without rework, repair or retest. The ratio is FPY for that step. Tracking this honestly (rather than counting reworked units as "passed") is the single biggest barrier to credible RTY numbers — and the most valuable.

What is a good RTY?

Above 95% is excellent for any multi-step process. 80-95% is typical for well-managed assemblies. Below 70% indicates either too many steps, too much rework or both, and is a strong signal to map the value stream end to end and remove or simplify steps.

Where should I focus when RTY is low?

Find the step with the largest defect contribution (the largest 1 - FPY weighted by step volume). Improving the worst step usually delivers more RTY uplift than improving any other. Plot a Pareto of step defects to make the choice obvious.
