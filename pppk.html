How it works

Understanding Pp and Ppk

Pp

Long-term potential

Pp uses the overall standard deviation — all data over time, including variation from shifts, batches, operators, and environment. It tells you how the process actually behaves in the long run, not just under ideal short-term conditions.

Ppk

Long-term actual

Ppk adds centering to Pp — it takes account of where the mean sits relative to the spec limits. A large gap between Pp and Ppk means the process is off-centre. A large gap between Cpk and Ppk means the process is unstable over time.

Pp/Cp

Stability ratio

Dividing Pp by Cp (or Ppk by Cpk) gives the stability ratio. A ratio close to 1.0 means the process is stable — long-term and short-term variation are similar. A ratio significantly below 1.0 indicates drift, shift, or instability between subgroups.

About this calculator

What this pp / ppk calculator does

Pp and Ppk are the long-term equivalents of Cp and Cpk. Where Cp/Cpk use the within-subgroup (short-term) standard deviation and capture only common-cause variation, Pp and Ppk use the overall standard deviation calculated from all individual values, capturing both within-subgroup variability and any drift between subgroups over time. This makes Pp/Ppk a more honest reflection of what the customer actually receives. This calculator takes your upper and lower spec limits, the process mean and the overall standard deviation, and returns Pp, Ppu, Ppl, Ppk, the predicted defect rate per million, and the gap between Cpk and Ppk (a direct measure of how unstable the process is).

When to use it

Use this calculator after a long-term capability study (typically 100+ measurements collected over multiple shifts, days or weeks), as a customer deliverable, when establishing baseline capability for a new product, or when investigating why customer-side defect rates exceed in-house ones. It is also the right tool when benchmarking real performance, not theoretical capability.

Worked example

A process has spec limits 495-505 ml, overall mean 500.3 ml and overall SD 1.6 ml (vs short-term SD 1.2 ml). Pp = (505 - 495) / (6 × 1.6) = 1.04. Ppu = (505 - 500.3) / (3 × 1.6) = 0.98; Ppl = 1.10; Ppk = 0.98. Compared to Cpk of 1.25 calculated from short-term data, Ppk of 0.98 reveals 27% additional drift between subgroups — the process is less stable than its short-term capability suggests, and the customer will see noticeably more out-of-spec parts than the Cpk number implies.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between Pp/Ppk and Cp/Cpk?

Cp/Cpk use the within-subgroup standard deviation (short-term, common-cause only). Pp/Ppk use the overall standard deviation calculated from every individual value (long-term, including drift and shift). Ppk is always less than or equal to Cpk; the gap is the size of the long-term instability.

Which one should I report to a customer?

Most customer requirements and AIAG standards specify Ppk for performance reporting because it represents what the customer actually receives. Cpk is reserved for short-term capability studies during development. Reporting Cpk where Ppk is required is one of the most common audit findings.

What does it mean when Cpk and Ppk are very different?

A large gap means the process is unstable — significant drift, shift or special-cause variation between subgroups. The short-term capability looks healthy but the long-term performance is much weaker. Stabilising the process (eliminating special causes) closes the gap before reducing variability further.

How much data do I need for a valid Ppk?

A minimum of 100 measurements collected over a representative time window (multiple shifts, multiple operators, multiple batches). Ppk based on 30 measurements collected in a single hour is statistically just a Cpk in disguise — it cannot capture the long-term drift Ppk is meant to reflect.

What Ppk is acceptable?

The same thresholds as Cpk: 1.33 capable, 1.67 highly capable, 2.0 six-sigma. The difference is that hitting Ppk = 1.67 is much harder than hitting Cpk = 1.67, because you must keep the process both narrow and centred over weeks or months, not just within a single subgroup.
