How it works

Attribute sampling explained

c=0

Zero-acceptance plans

The c=0 rule means: sample n items, accept the lot only if zero defects are found. The formula n = log(α) / log(1−p) gives the sample size that provides (1−α)% confidence that the true defect rate is below p when no defects are found. It is simple, conservative, and widely adopted in ISO and automotive quality standards.

CI

Confidence intervals for proportions

A confidence interval tells you the range in which the true defect rate most likely falls, given what you observed. A 95% CI does not mean there is a 95% chance the true rate falls in the interval — it means: if you repeated the sampling many times, 95% of the constructed intervals would contain the true rate.

DPMO

Defects per million opportunities

DPMO = (defects / (n × opportunities)) × 1,000,000. It standardises the defect rate to a per-million scale, allowing comparison across processes with different complexity. A DPMO of 3,400 corresponds to 6 Sigma; 66,800 is 3 Sigma; 308,537 is 2 Sigma. Lower DPMO means better quality.

About this calculator

What this attribute sample size calculator does

When the data is binary — defective or good, converted or not, pass or fail — sample size is calculated from a different formula than for continuous data. The standard formula is n = (Z² × p × (1-p)) ÷ E², where p is the estimated proportion, Z is the confidence-level multiplier and E is the desired margin of error. This calculator takes the three inputs, returns the minimum sample size required, and shows the impact of changing each one. If you do not know p in advance, the calculator uses the conservative worst-case value of p = 0.5, which gives the largest sample requirement and the safest plan.

When to use it

Use this calculator before any attribute sampling exercise: defect-rate audits, conversion-rate experiments, customer satisfaction yes/no surveys, supplier acceptance sampling, MSA gauge R&R for go/no-go gauges, or political-style polling. It is also the right tool when a stakeholder demands a tighter margin and you need to show the cost in extra inspections or extra survey responses.

Worked example

You need to estimate a defect rate to within ±1% at 95% confidence, with a best-guess of 5% defects. n = (1.96² × 0.05 × 0.95) ÷ 0.01² = 1,825 units. If you have no prior estimate of p, use p = 0.5 → n = (1.96² × 0.25) ÷ 0.01² = 9,604 units — five times more. Loosening the margin to ±2% with the original 5% estimate drops n to 457; loosening confidence to 90% drops it to 1,288. Small changes in margin or confidence dramatically change cost.

Frequently asked questions

Why does p = 0.5 give the largest sample size?

The variance of a binomial proportion is p × (1-p), which is maximised at p = 0.5 (variance = 0.25). For any other value of p the variance is smaller and the required sample size is smaller too. p = 0.5 is therefore the conservative worst case — safe to use when no prior estimate exists.

How is attribute sample size different from continuous?

Attribute samples need many more observations than continuous samples for the same precision because each observation carries less information (one bit, vs a real number). A factor-of-10 to factor-of-100 difference is typical between sample sizes for proportions and means.

What confidence level should I choose?

95% is the default for most studies (Z = 1.96). 90% (Z = 1.645) is acceptable for exploratory work. 99% (Z = 2.58) is reserved for safety-critical or regulatory applications where the cost of a false conclusion is high. Each step up multiplies sample size meaningfully.

Does the population size matter?

For populations larger than ~10,000, no — the formula assumes infinite population. For smaller populations, apply the finite population correction: n_corrected = n / (1 + (n-1)/N), which can substantially reduce the required sample for small populations like internal audit lots.

How does this relate to AQL sampling plans?

AQL plans (e.g. ANSI Z1.4) use this binomial maths under the hood but additionally specify accept/reject numbers and switching rules between normal, tightened and reduced inspection. Use this calculator for the underlying sample-size logic; use a published AQL plan when you need a defensible standardised plan.
