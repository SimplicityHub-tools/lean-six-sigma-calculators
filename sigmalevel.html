Understanding Sigma Levels

1

The 1.5 Sigma shift

Six Sigma uses a 1.5 sigma shift to account for real-world process drift over time. This is why a 6 Sigma process allows 3.4 DPMO rather than zero.

2

Yield and defects

Yield is the percentage of outputs that are defect-free. A 3 Sigma process has a yield of around 93.3%. A 6 Sigma process achieves 99.9997% yield.

What to do next

About this calculator

What this sigma level calculator does

The Sigma Level is the headline Six Sigma performance metric. It expresses defect rate on a scale where every step represents an order-of-magnitude improvement: 3σ ≈ 6.7% defects, 4σ ≈ 0.62%, 5σ ≈ 0.023%, 6σ = 3.4 defects per million opportunities (DPMO). The metric is commonly reported with an inbuilt 1.5σ "shift" to convert short-term capability into expected long-term performance — so a long-term Sigma Level of 6 is calculated from a short-term Z of 7.5. This calculator takes defects, units and opportunities, converts them to DPMO, and returns both the short-term and long-term Sigma Level, the yield, and the equivalent Cpk for context.

When to use it

Use this calculator any time a process needs to be reported on a Six Sigma scorecard, when comparing capability across products of different complexity, when setting an improvement target ("we are at 4σ — get us to 5σ"), or when answering the inevitable executive question "how many sigma is that?". It is also the right tool when converting defect-count audit findings into a single benchmarkable number.

Worked example

A finished assembly contains 25 inspection opportunities. In 500 units inspected, 48 defects are found. DPMO = 48 ÷ (500 × 25) × 1,000,000 = 3,840. Long-term Sigma Level ≈ 4.16 (better than industry average of 4σ ≈ 6,210 DPMO, well short of world-class 6σ = 3.4 DPMO). Halving defects to 24 lifts the long-term Sigma Level to about 4.4. Reaching a true 5σ process means dropping below 233 DPMO — fewer than 12 defects per 500 units inspected.

Frequently asked questions

What is a Sigma Level?

Sigma Level is a single-number summary of defect rate, expressed in standard deviations of the underlying normal distribution. Higher is better. Every additional sigma represents roughly an order-of-magnitude improvement in defect rate, all the way down to the 3.4 DPMO at 6σ.

Why is there a 1.5σ shift in the table?

Empirical work at Motorola in the 1980s showed that long-term process means typically drift by about 1.5σ relative to short-term performance. The shift converts a short-term capability index into the expected long-term defect rate the customer actually sees.

How does Sigma Level relate to Cpk?

Sigma Level = 3 × Cpk for the short-term (one-sided) view. So a Cpk of 1.33 corresponds to short-term Sigma ≈ 4.0; Cpk of 1.67 ≈ 5.0; Cpk of 2.0 = 6.0. The long-term Sigma Level subtracts the 1.5σ shift, so the same Cpk of 2.0 corresponds to a long-term Sigma Level of 4.5.

What Sigma Level should I aim for?

Industry average is around 4σ. Most Six Sigma programmes target 5σ as a realistic medium-term goal (≈233 DPMO long-term). A genuine 6σ is reserved for safety-critical processes — aerospace, medical, semiconductor — where 3.4 DPMO is required. The economic optimum for most processes is 4.5-5.5σ.

Can Sigma Level be calculated for service processes?

Yes, and it is one of the most powerful applications. Define an opportunity as any element of a transaction that can be wrong (correct address, on time, complete, accurate). Service operations typically start at 2-3σ and reach 4-5σ with focused improvement work — turning service quality into a number that can be benchmarked against manufacturing.
