How it works

Why sample size matters

CI

Confidence & margin of error

The confidence level (e.g. 95%) is the probability that your interval contains the true value if you repeated the study many times. The margin of error is half the width of that interval. A wider margin means a less precise estimate but a smaller sample. Tighter precision always costs more samples.

1-β

Power (two-sample comparisons)

Statistical power is the probability of correctly detecting a real difference between two groups. Low power means you risk missing real improvements — a false negative (Type II error). Most studies target 80–90% power. Higher power requires larger samples, especially when the difference you want to detect is small.

n²

Diminishing returns

Halving the margin of error requires quadrupling the sample size (the relationship is quadratic, not linear). This is why the trade-off chart is curved. Beyond a certain point, collecting more data yields very little additional precision — and the chart shows exactly where that inflection point occurs for your specific inputs.

About this calculator

What this sample size calculator does

The right sample size is the difference between a study that proves something and one that wastes weeks of effort to be inconclusive. For estimating a population mean, the required sample size is driven by three things: the margin of error you can tolerate (E), the confidence level you need (typically 95% or 99%), and your best estimate of the population standard deviation (σ). This calculator combines them using the standard formula n = (Z × σ ÷ E)², rounds up to the nearest whole number, and returns the minimum sample size required. It also shows the impact of changing each input so you can see whether a tighter margin or higher confidence is worth the extra data collection cost.

When to use it

Use this calculator before a measurement system analysis, capability study, customer satisfaction survey, audit, factor screening or any data-collection plan. It is also the right tool when a project sponsor pushes for "more accuracy" — the calculator instantly shows that halving the margin requires roughly four times the sample.

Worked example

A team needs to estimate the mean fill weight of a bottling line to within ±0.3 g at 95% confidence. From historical data σ ≈ 1.5 g. n = (1.96 × 1.5 ÷ 0.3)² = 96.04 → 97 bottles. Tightening the margin to ±0.2 g would push the sample to 217 bottles. Lifting confidence to 99% with the original ±0.3 g margin would push it to 166. The calculator makes the cost of each tightening immediately visible.

Frequently asked questions

What inputs do I need for sample size for a mean?

Three inputs: the margin of error you can tolerate (E), the confidence level (typically 90%, 95% or 99%), and your best estimate of the population standard deviation. The standard deviation usually comes from a pilot study, historical data or a published benchmark.

What if I do not know the population standard deviation?

Run a small pilot study (20-30 observations) to estimate σ, use the highest plausible value (a conservative upper bound), or use a published benchmark for similar processes. The penalty for guessing too low is an underpowered study; the penalty for guessing too high is over-collecting data.

How does halving the margin of error affect sample size?

Sample size is inversely proportional to the square of the margin: halving E quadruples n. Doubling the margin reduces n by a factor of four. This square-law relationship is the single biggest driver of data-collection cost in any quantitative study.

How does confidence level affect sample size?

Higher confidence requires a larger sample. Going from 95% to 99% confidence (Z = 1.96 → 2.58) multiplies n by about 1.73 for the same margin. Going from 95% to 90% (Z = 1.96 → 1.645) shrinks n by about 30%.

Is this calculator suitable for proportions?

No. For estimating a proportion (e.g. defect rate, conversion rate), the formula uses p × (1-p) instead of σ². Use a dedicated attribute sample size calculator (we provide one) — using the mean formula on proportion data will give the wrong answer.
