How it works

The binomial distribution explained

PMF

Probability mass function

P(X=k) = C(n,k) ⋅ p² ⋅ (1−p)^(n−k). This gives the probability of exactly k successes (or defects) in n independent trials when each trial has probability p of success. The binomial requires: fixed n, independent trials, constant p, binary outcomes.

CDF

Cumulative probabilities

P(X ≤ k) is the sum of all PMF values from 0 to k — the probability of at most k successes. P(X ≥ k) = 1 − P(X ≤ k−1). These are essential for acceptance sampling decisions: "what is the probability of accepting a lot with c or fewer defects?"

6σ

Six Sigma applications

Attribute sampling uses the binomial to evaluate acceptance plans. If you inspect n items from a lot with defect rate p, the probability of finding k or fewer defects is P(X ≤ k). Setting k=0 gives the c=0 plan probability. Compare this to the producer's risk (α) and consumer's risk (β) to evaluate plan quality.

About this calculator

What this binomial distribution calculator does

The binomial distribution describes the number of successes in n independent trials, each with the same probability of success p. It is the foundation of attribute sampling, defect-rate calculations, and almost every yes/no quality decision. This calculator takes the three inputs (n, p and k), and returns the exact probability of exactly k successes, the cumulative probability of k or fewer successes, the upper-tail probability of k or more, the mean (n × p), variance (n × p × (1-p)) and standard deviation. It also plots the probability mass function so you can see whether the distribution is skewed, near-symmetric, or so close to a Z-distribution that the normal approximation could be used.

When to use it

Use this calculator any time you have a fixed number of independent trials with a constant success/failure probability: acceptance sampling plans, conversion-rate forecasting, defect-occurrence probability, tolerance interval calculations, or simulating outcomes for a project decision. It is also the right tool when sizing an attribute sampling plan ("how many can I inspect and still see at least one defect?").

Worked example

A process has a 2% defect rate. In a sample of n = 100 units, what is the probability of finding at least 3 defects? n = 100, p = 0.02, k = 3. P(X ≥ 3) = 1 - P(X ≤ 2) = 1 - (0.1326 + 0.2707 + 0.2734) = 1 - 0.6767 = 0.3233. So there is about a 32% chance of seeing 3+ defects in 100 units even though the long-run rate is only 2% — a single sample is noisier than people expect, and is the reason single-shot judgements about defect rates are so often wrong.

Frequently asked questions

When does the binomial distribution apply?

When you have (1) a fixed number of trials n, (2) only two outcomes per trial — success or failure, (3) constant probability of success p across trials, and (4) independence between trials. Most attribute sampling problems satisfy these assumptions to a useful approximation.

What are the mean and variance of a binomial distribution?

Mean = n × p. Variance = n × p × (1-p). Standard deviation is the square root of the variance. For n = 100, p = 0.05, mean = 5, variance = 4.75, SD ≈ 2.18 — so the realistic range of outcomes spans roughly 0-12 defects despite the long-run mean of 5.

When can I use the normal approximation?

The normal approximation is reasonable when both n × p ≥ 5 and n × (1-p) ≥ 5. Below those thresholds, especially for low p, use the exact binomial or the Poisson approximation. Reaching for the normal too early underestimates tail probabilities — exactly where defect risk lives.

How does the binomial differ from the Poisson?

Binomial counts successes in a fixed number of trials with a known probability. Poisson counts events in a fixed window of time, area or opportunity, with a known average rate. The Poisson is the limit of the binomial as n grows large and p shrinks, with n × p held constant.

What is acceptance sampling and how does the binomial fit in?

Acceptance sampling decides whether to accept or reject a lot based on a small inspected sample. The binomial is the underlying probability model: given an assumed defect rate p, it tells you the probability of seeing at most k defects in a sample of n — which is exactly what an OC curve plots.
