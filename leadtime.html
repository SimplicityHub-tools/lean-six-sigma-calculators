How it works

Understanding Little's Law

1

The universal flow law

Little's Law states that average lead time equals average WIP divided by average throughput. It applies to any stable system — factories, hospitals, software teams, call centres — making it the most widely-used flow equation in Lean.

2

Why WIP is the lever

If throughput is fixed by your process capability, the only way to reduce lead time is to reduce WIP. Halve the WIP, halve the lead time. This is why visual boards, WIP limits and Kanban work so powerfully.

3

Set realistic promises

Use this lead time as the basis for delivery dates and customer promises. If a new item joins the queue today, expect it to take roughly this long to come out the other side — assuming no priority changes or capacity shifts.

About this calculator

What this lead time calculator does

Lead time is the total elapsed time from the moment a customer order arrives to the moment the finished product is delivered. It is dominated by waiting time, not working time. Most value streams spend less than 5% of total lead time actually adding value — the other 95% is queues, batches, transport, inspection waits, approvals and rework. This calculator takes the process time and queue time of each step in your value stream, sums them into a total lead time, returns the value-add ratio (process time ÷ lead time), and ranks the steps by the size of their queue time so you can see where the waiting waste lives. Reducing lead time almost always means attacking queues, not speeding up working steps.

When to use it

Use this calculator after a value-stream-mapping workshop, before quoting a customer lead time, when investigating why lead times are slipping, or when sizing the prize from a flow-improvement project. It is also the right tool when a sales team is pushing for a shorter quote and you need to show what really drives the elapsed-time number.

Worked example

A six-step process has process times of 5, 8, 3, 10, 7 and 6 minutes (total = 39 minutes), with queue times of 60, 240, 30, 480, 120 and 90 minutes (total = 1,020 minutes). Total lead time = 1,059 minutes (~17.7 hours); value-add ratio = 39 ÷ 1,059 = 3.7%. Step 4 alone (queue 480 minutes) is 47% of the total wait. Halving the queue at step 4 would cut total lead time by 22% with no change to any working step — a classic queue-attack opportunity that cycle-time projects routinely miss.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between lead time and cycle time?

Cycle time is how long one step takes to process one unit. Lead time is how long the entire value stream takes one unit, including all queues and waits between steps. Lead time is almost always many times larger than the sum of cycle times — the gap is queue time.

What is value-add ratio?

Value-add ratio = total process time ÷ total lead time. Most untouched value streams sit at 1-5%. Lean operations get to 10-25%. Anything above 30% is exceptional and usually involves cell-based flow with single-piece movement and no batching.

How do I reduce lead time without speeding up the steps?

Attack queues. Reduce batch size, level the production schedule, balance the line so work-in-process does not pool at the slowest step, eliminate waits for approval or inspection, and pull rather than push between steps. Most lead-time projects deliver 50%+ reduction without changing any process speed.

Why does shorter lead time matter to the business?

Shorter lead times reduce inventory, free up cash, improve forecast accuracy (because forecasts can be shorter), reduce rework triggered by demand changes during long lead times, and let the business win quote-driven business that competitors with longer lead times cannot.

Should lead time include transport between sites?

Yes. Lead time is end-to-end as the customer experiences it — from order placed to order delivered. Transport between sites, customs delays, port waits and last-mile delivery are all part of the lead time the customer sees, even though they are usually invisible to operations dashboards.
