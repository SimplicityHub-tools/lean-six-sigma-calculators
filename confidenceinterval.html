Understanding Confidence Interval

1

What a CI tells you

A 95% confidence interval gives you the range that the true population value is likely to fall within, with 95% confidence. Wider intervals mean more uncertainty; narrower intervals mean more precision — but require larger sample sizes.

2

Mean vs proportion

For continuous data (heights, weights, cycle times) use the mean mode. For pass/fail or yes/no data (defective rate, conversion rate, customer satisfaction %) use the proportion mode — the maths is different.

3

The cost of confidence

Going from 95% to 99% confidence widens your interval by about 30% — meaning more uncertainty for the same data. To narrow the interval, the only lever is sample size: doubling n cuts the margin of error by about 30%.

About this calculator

What this confidence interval calculator does

A confidence interval is a range that is likely to contain the true population parameter, given a sample drawn from that population. A 95% confidence interval means: if we repeated the sampling many times, 95% of the resulting intervals would capture the true value. This calculator handles both modes — a confidence interval for a mean (using the sample mean and sample standard deviation) and a confidence interval for a proportion (using the count of successes and the sample size). Set any confidence level between 50% and 99.99% and the tool returns the margin of error, the lower bound, the upper bound, and a width indicator showing how precise the estimate is.

When to use it

Use this calculator whenever you have a sample-based estimate and need to communicate its uncertainty: customer satisfaction surveys, defect-rate samples, conversion-rate tests, audit results, batch testing, or any A/B comparison. It is also the right tool when stakeholders treat a single survey number as exact — the CI puts honest bounds on it.

Worked example

A survey of 100 customers gives a mean satisfaction score of 50 with sample SD of 8. At 95% confidence, the margin of error is 1.96 × 8 ÷ √100 = 1.568. The 95% CI is 48.43 to 51.57. To halve that margin you would need roughly four times the sample size (n = 400). For a proportion: 320 conversions in 1000 visitors gives p = 32% with a 95% CI of roughly 29.1% to 34.9%.

Frequently asked questions

What is a confidence interval?

A confidence interval is a range of values calculated from a sample that is likely to contain the true population value, with a stated confidence level (typically 95%). It is the standard way to report uncertainty around an estimate.

What does 95% confidence actually mean?

It means that if you repeated the sampling and CI calculation many times, 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true population value. It does not mean there is a 95% chance the true value is in any one specific interval — that is a common misinterpretation.

When do I use a CI for a mean versus a proportion?

Use the mean mode for continuous data (heights, weights, cycle times, scores). Use the proportion mode for binary data (pass/fail, yes/no, defective/good, converted/not converted). The underlying maths is different.

How does confidence level affect interval width?

Higher confidence widens the interval. Going from 95% to 99% widens the CI by about 30% on the same data. Higher confidence is not "better" — it is a trade-off between certainty and precision.

How do I narrow a confidence interval?

The only lever is sample size. Doubling n shrinks the margin of error by about 30% (a factor of √2). Quadrupling n halves it. Reducing variability in the data also helps, but is usually outside the analyst's control.
