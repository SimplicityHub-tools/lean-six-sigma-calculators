About this calculator

What this dataset analysis calculator does

Descriptive statistics are the first thing every analyst, Black Belt and quality engineer reaches for when handed a new dataset. They condense dozens or thousands of raw values into a handful of numbers that describe the centre, the spread and the shape of the distribution. This calculator takes a paste-in list of numbers and returns the count, sum, minimum, maximum, range, mean, median, mode, sample standard deviation, variance, first and third quartiles, interquartile range, skewness and kurtosis — alongside a histogram so you can see the shape, not just the summary statistics. Together these tell you whether the data is symmetric, skewed, unimodal, bimodal or contains obvious outliers.

When to use it

Use this calculator any time you receive a fresh sample and need to understand it before doing anything else: capability studies, hypothesis tests, control charting, audit findings, survey results or KPI dashboards. It is also the right tool when a colleague hands you a column of numbers and asks "what does this look like?" — the histogram answers in two seconds what a list of numbers cannot.

Worked example

A sample of 20 cycle times yields a mean of 45.2s and a median of 44.8s — close enough that the data looks roughly symmetric. The standard deviation is 4.1s, giving a coefficient of variation of 9% (low). Q1 = 42.5s, Q3 = 47.9s, IQR = 5.4s. Skewness is +0.18 (very mild right skew) and kurtosis is -0.4 (slightly flatter than normal). One value at 58s sits more than 1.5×IQR above Q3 and is flagged as a possible outlier worth investigating before further analysis.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between mean and median?

The mean is the arithmetic average; the median is the middle value when the data is sorted. They are equal in a perfectly symmetric distribution. When they diverge, the data is skewed — and the median is usually the more honest measure of "typical".

When should I use sample versus population standard deviation?

Use the sample SD (dividing by n-1) for any data that is a sample drawn from a larger population, which is almost every real dataset. Use the population SD (dividing by n) only when you have measured every member of the population, which is rare.

What does the interquartile range tell me?

The IQR is the spread of the middle 50% of the data (Q3 minus Q1). It is robust to outliers — unlike the standard deviation — and is the basis of the standard 1.5×IQR rule for flagging outliers in box plots and exploratory analysis.

What is skewness and why does it matter?

Skewness measures asymmetry. Positive skew means a long right tail (a few large values pulling the mean above the median). Negative skew means a long left tail. Skewness above |1| typically rules out the assumption of normality required by t-tests, ANOVA and capability indices.

How big a sample do I need for reliable statistics?

For a stable mean, 30 observations is the rule of thumb. For a stable standard deviation, 50-100 is safer. For percentile and tail-shape statistics (skewness, kurtosis, capability), 100-200 observations is the practical minimum.
