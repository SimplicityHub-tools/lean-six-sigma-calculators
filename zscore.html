Understanding Z-Score

1

What a Z-score means

A Z-score expresses how many standard deviations a value is from the mean. A z of +1 means one SD above the mean (84th percentile in a normal distribution). A z of -2 means two SDs below (2.3rd percentile). It standardises any value to a comparable scale.

2

Why standardise

Z-scores let you compare values from completely different distributions on equal terms — for example, a candidate scoring 75 in one test and 80 in another. The Z-score tells you which performance was actually better relative to its peer group.

3

Six Sigma link

Six Sigma uses Z-scores directly: the Sigma Level is just the Z-score above which 99.99966% of process output falls. A 6σ process produces only 3.4 defects per million opportunities — that's z = 4.5 (long-term) or z = 6 (short-term).

About this calculator

What this z-score calculator does

A Z-score expresses how many standard deviations a single value is from the mean of its distribution. It is the most-used standardisation in statistics and the foundation of Six Sigma capability metrics. A Z-score of +1 means one standard deviation above the mean (84th percentile in a normal distribution). A Z-score of -2 means two SDs below (2.3rd percentile). A Z-score of zero is exactly at the mean. This calculator takes a raw value, the mean and the standard deviation, and returns the Z-score, the cumulative probability below the value, the probability above it, and the position plotted on the standard normal curve.

When to use it

Use this calculator whenever you need to compare values from different distributions on equal terms (test scores, quality measurements, financial returns), assess how unusual an observation is, calculate the area under the normal curve for a hypothesis test, or convert a process capability index into a Sigma Level. It is also the right tool when a colleague asks "is this result actually significant or just noise?".

Worked example

A candidate scores 75 on a test where the mean is 60 and the standard deviation is 8. Z = (75 - 60) ÷ 8 = 1.875. The percentile is 96.97% — meaning the candidate scored higher than 97% of the population. Compared to a second candidate who scored 80 on a different test (mean 70, SD 12, Z = 0.83, 79th percentile), the first candidate clearly performed better relative to peers, despite the lower raw score.

Frequently asked questions

What does a Z-score tell you?

A Z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value sits above or below the mean. Positive Z-scores are above the mean, negative are below, zero is exactly on the mean. The further from zero, the more unusual the value.

What is the formula for a Z-score?

Z = (x - μ) / σ, where x is the value, μ is the population mean, and σ is the population standard deviation. For a sample, substitute the sample mean and sample SD.

What Z-score is considered significant?

In hypothesis testing, |Z| ≥ 1.96 is significant at the 95% level (two-tailed), |Z| ≥ 2.58 at the 99% level. In Six Sigma, a process Z (sigma level) of 6 represents 3.4 defects per million opportunities — the long-term world-class benchmark.

How do you convert a Z-score to a percentile?

A Z-score is converted to a percentile using the standard normal cumulative distribution function, Φ(Z). This calculator does it automatically. Z = 0 is the 50th percentile; Z = +1 is the 84th; Z = -1 is the 16th.

What is the link between Z-score and Sigma Level?

In Six Sigma, the Sigma Level of a process is the Z-score for which the process meets specification 99.99966% of the time. A 6-sigma process has Z = 6 short-term (Z = 4.5 long-term, allowing for the 1.5σ shift), producing only 3.4 defects per million opportunities.
