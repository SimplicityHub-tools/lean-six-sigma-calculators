How it works

Understanding DPMO

D

Defects

The total number of defects found across all units in your sample. A defect is any instance where a unit fails to meet a specification.

U

Units

The total number of items, products, or transactions inspected or produced during the measurement period.

O

Opportunities

The number of ways each unit could potentially have a defect. A form with 10 fields has 10 opportunities for error per unit.

About this calculator

What this dpmo calculator does

Defects Per Million Opportunities (DPMO) is the standard normalised quality metric in Six Sigma. Unlike a simple defect rate, DPMO accounts for how many opportunities for a defect exist in each unit — so a complex product with 50 defect opportunities per unit can be compared fairly against a simple one with 5. The formula is (defects ÷ (units × opportunities)) × 1,000,000. This calculator takes the three inputs, returns the DPMO figure, the defect rate per unit, the yield, and the equivalent short-term and long-term Sigma Level (the long-term figure includes the standard 1.5σ shift used in most Six Sigma reporting).

When to use it

Use this calculator any time you need to express quality on a scale that compares fairly across products of different complexity, report capability to a customer or auditor, set an improvement target, or convert between defect rate and Sigma Level for a project tollgate. It is also the right tool when a stakeholder asks "how many sigma is that?".

Worked example

A finished assembly contains 25 inspection opportunities. Out of 500 units inspected, 48 defects are found. DPMO = 48 ÷ (500 × 25) × 1,000,000 = 3,840. That maps to a long-term Sigma Level of about 4.16 — better than industry average (≈4.0, around 6,210 DPMO) but well short of world-class (6σ, 3.4 DPMO). Halving the defect rate to 24 defects would lift Sigma to about 4.4; getting to a true 5σ process would mean fewer than 12 defects per 500 units.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between DPMO and DPU?

DPU is defects per unit (defects ÷ units). DPMO normalises by opportunity count and scales to one million, so it is comparable across products of different complexity. A 50-component PCB with the same DPU as a 5-component bracket is actually 10x better in DPMO terms.

How do I count opportunities correctly?

An opportunity is any feature that could be defective and is independently inspected — a hole, a weld, a label position, a paint surface, a torque value. Be consistent across products: inflating opportunity counts artificially boosts DPMO and is a classic gaming pattern auditors look for.

What is a good DPMO?

6σ = 3.4 DPMO long-term (world-class). 5σ ≈ 233 DPMO. 4σ ≈ 6,210 DPMO (typical industry). 3σ ≈ 66,807 DPMO (poor; about 6.7%). Most untouched processes start in the 3-4σ range; the journey from 4σ to 5σ usually takes 12-24 months of focused improvement work.

Why do Sigma tables include a 1.5σ shift?

Long-term processes drift; the 1.5σ shift is the empirical adjustment Motorola introduced to convert short-term capability into expected long-term performance. Long-term Z = short-term Z minus 1.5. The 3.4 DPMO figure for "six sigma" assumes this shift is included.

Can DPMO be used for service processes?

Yes, and it is one of the most valuable applications. Define an opportunity as any element of a transaction that can be wrong (correct address, correct amount, on time, complete). DPMO turns service quality into a number that can be benchmarked against manufacturing six sigma.
